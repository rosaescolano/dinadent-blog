Feeling a sharp, sudden pain when drinking hot coffee, eating ice cream, or even breathing in cold air is a very common symptom known as dentin hypersensitivity. This issue affects a high percentage of the population and, although often ignored, is a warning sign that the protective layer of your teeth is wearing away.

Under normal conditions, dentin (the inner layer surrounding the tooth nerve) is protected by enamel on the crown and by the gum on the root. Dentin contains thousands of microscopic tubules filled with fluid that connect directly to nerve endings. When enamel wears down or the gum recedes, these tubules are exposed to the outside. Thermal, chemical, or tactile stimuli cause the fluid to move sharply, triggering the nerves and producing that immediate pain sensation.

The most frequent causes of sensitivity include overly aggressive brushing with hard-bristled toothbrushes, the unsupervised use of highly abrasive whitening toothpastes, bruxism (teeth grinding due to stress), and excessive consumption of acidic foods that erode enamel. Clinical treatment options include the application of high-concentration desensitizing gels, tubule sealants, or, if there is gum recession, composite restorations to cover the exposed root and restore patient comfort.
